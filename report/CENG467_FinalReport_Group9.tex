\title{Unsupervised Neural Machine Translation for Turkish and English: Denoising Autoencoder and Iterative Back-Translation}
\author{Group 9}
\institute{CENG467 Term Project}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This report details the design, implementation, and evaluation of an Unsupervised Neural Machine Translation (UNMT) system for the Turkish-English language pair, developed without using any parallel corpora. We leverage a shared lightweight Transformer encoder-decoder architecture (46M parameters) trained initially as a Denoising Autoencoder (DAE) to model language-specific distributions, followed by Iterative Back-Translation (IBT) to align the latent spaces. Our implementation builds heavily upon the methodologies proposed by Lample et al. (2018). We present extensive evaluations on the FLORES-200 benchmark, comparing our unsupervised approach against a word-by-word fastText baseline and a supervised Helsinki-NLP reference model. Detailed error analyses highlight the specific challenges posed by Turkish agglutinative morphology in an unsupervised setting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Neural Machine Translation (NMT) has achieved near-human parity on resource-rich language pairs by relying on massive parallel corpora. However, parallel data is inherently scarce, expensive to create, and unavailable for many language combinations. Unsupervised Neural Machine Translation (UNMT) addresses this by training models solely on monolingual corpora. In this project, we explore the challenges of applying UNMT to the Turkish-English pair, a morphologically rich and structurally distinct combination.

\section{Related Work}
Lample et al. (2018) introduced a robust framework for UNMT relying on three core principles: 1) shared latent representations, 2) denoising autoencoding, and 3) iterative back-translation. Subsequent works such as XLM and mBART extended this by pre-training massive masked language models. Due to computational constraints, our project replicates the fundamental principles of Lample et al. using a lightweight Transformer.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Data Preprocessing and Vocabulary}
We initially utilized 1,000,000 sentences per language from the CC-100 dataset. However, due to computational bottlenecks during the iterative back-translation phase, we later sub-sampled the dataset to 200,000 sentences per language to allow for a higher number of training iterations. We trained a joint SentencePiece Unigram vocabulary (size 32,000) on both corpora to enforce a shared embedding space. Turkish lowercasing and aggressive web-noise filtering were applied.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
A 4-layer, 8-head shared Transformer Encoder-Decoder (46M parameters) was implemented using PyTorch. The embedding layer is shared across both languages and tied to the output projection. A language identifier token (\texttt{<TR>} or \texttt{<EN>}) prepended to the encoder input signals the target language.

\subsection{Denoising Autoencoder (DAE)}
To establish a strong language model prior, we trained the network to reconstruct clean sentences from corrupted inputs. The noise function applies word dropping ($p=0.1$), word masking ($p=0.1$), and local word shuffling ($k=3$).

\subsection{Iterative Back-Translation (IBT)}
The core translation capability emerges during the IBT phase. The model generates synthetic parallel data by translating monolingual sentences using greedy decoding. The model is then trained with a cross-entropy objective to predict the real sentence from the synthetic translation.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\subsection{Baselines}
We established three baselines: 1) A Copy Baseline, 2) A Word-by-Word translation using MUSE-aligned fastText embeddings, and 3) A fully supervised Helsinki-NLP MarianMT model.

\subsection{Training Details}
Training was conducted on a Google Colab A100 GPU using FP16 mixed precision and gradient accumulation to simulate a large batch size of 128. AdamW was utilized with a cosine learning rate scheduler. 

Our training progressed in two distinct phases due to dataset size constraints. In our initial attempt using 1,000,000 sentences, the Denoising Autoencoder (DAE) pre-training successfully converged to a best cross-entropy loss of 5.15 after 3 epochs. However, a single Iterative Back-Translation (IBT) iteration took approximately 4 hours, preventing the model from properly aligning the latent spaces. To remedy this, we reduced the dataset size to 200,000 sentences. In this optimized setup, DAE pre-training was extended to 10 epochs (achieving a loss of 6.14), which allowed us to run 10 full IBT iterations.

\section{Error Analysis}
We manually annotated 50 randomly sampled sentences. The dominant error categories were:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Syntax Mismatch:} Subject-Object-Verb (Turkish) vs. Subject-Verb-Object (English) alignment failures.
    \item \textbf{Hallucination:} The model reverting to auto-encoding behavior or generating fluent but unrelated text.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
Our experiments highlight the extreme difficulty of training Unsupervised NMT models from scratch on morphologically distant languages like Turkish and English. Although increasing the IBT iterations from 1 to 10 led to a slight improvement in TR $\rightarrow$ EN translation (0.09 to 0.17 BLEU), the model fundamentally failed to surpass the Word-by-Word baseline (3.25 BLEU).

This negative result is highly instructive and stems from two major bottlenecks:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data and Parameter Starvation:} The original framework by Lample et al. relies on tens of millions of sentences and hundreds of millions of parameters. Our lightweight model (46M parameters) trained on 200,000 sentences simply lacks the capacity and the distributional diversity to perfectly align two entirely distinct latent spaces from scratch.
    \item \textbf{The Strength of Pre-trained Embeddings:} The Word-by-Word baseline utilizes MUSE embeddings, which are trained on billions of words using fastText and aligned via adversarial networks. Our model attempts to learn this alignment \textit{ex nihilo} (from nothing) using only the DAE and IBT signals, which is mathematically much harder.
\end{enumerate}

These findings confirm the modern NLP consensus that purely unsupervised NMT from scratch is too unstable for distant language pairs, and massive cross-lingual pre-training (e.g., XLM, mBART) is an absolute necessity to initialize the shared latent space.

\section{Conclusion}
This project successfully implemented a complete UNMT pipeline, exploring the trade-offs between dataset size and iteration counts. While reaching the performance of supervised systems or even strong dictionary-based baselines remains challenging under resource constraints, the methodology demonstrates the theoretical framework of latent space alignment via Iterative Back-Translation. Future work must leverage massive pre-trained cross-lingual language models to overcome the initial alignment barrier.
